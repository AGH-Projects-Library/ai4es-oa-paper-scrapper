# Semi-Automated Generation and Hemodynamic Assessment of Surgical Baffle Geometry for Biventricular Repair

Patient-specific computational modeling has emerged as a powerful tool for surgical planning in complex congenital heart disease. One promising application is complex biventricular repair, which often requires construction of a custom intraventricular baffle to establish a physiologic left ventricle-to-aorta outflow pathway. In current practice, baffle geometry is designed and shaped intraoperatively and preoperative planning remains largely manual, limiting the ability to generate anatomically conformal, watertight models suitable for quantitative hemodynamic assessment. In this work, we present a semi-automated computational framework for the design and assessment of patient-specific intraventricular baffles. The method constructs an explicit VSD-to-aorta flow pathway, preserves native right ventricular geometry, and reshapes only the baffle region using section-wise area constraints along a physiologically aligned centerline. The resulting geometry is integrated into a closed, multi-labeled domain for computational fluid dynamics (CFD) analysis. We retrospectively applied this framework to four patients with double outlet right ventricle (DORV) who previously underwent biventricular repair. For each case, a patient-specific baffle was generated and its hemodynamic performance was evaluated using CFD. Predicted pressure gradients across the reconstructed outflow were within clinically acceptable ranges and comparable to the patients’ postoperative echocardiographs. This approach enables quantitative, pre-operative design and evaluation of candidate baffle geometries and provides a reproducible method for generating simulation-ready models. By combining physiologically constrained geometric design with CFD-based assessment, the framework represents a step toward computational, patient-specific decision support for biventricular flow restoration in a complex heterogeneous patient population.

Keywords: computational cardiology, biventricular repair, virtual surgery planning, double outlet right ventricle


## 1 Introduction

Congenital heart defects (CHDs) are the most common type of birth defect, affecting roughly 40,000 births annually in the United States. They encompass a broad and highly variable spectrum of structural abnormalities [ 11 ] . Many patients with severe defects have only one ventricle effectively contributing to cardiac output and therefore require surgical intervention to restore adequate circulation. Historically, these highly heterogeneous patients were often palliated to a single-ventricle physiology [ 32 ] . However, this strategy is associated with substantial long-term morbidity and mortality. Therefore, when anatomically feasible, several advanced centers offer complex biventricular repair as an alternative strategy that restores two-ventricle physiology [ 16 , 3 ] .

In parallel, computational modeling has emerged as a powerful tool for generating patient-specific predictions across a range of clinical applications [ 41 , 28 , 44 , 48 , 54 ] , and supporting clinical decision making, with particular impact in pediatric cardiology [ 64 , 30 , 73 , 39 , 17 , 40 , 31 , 31 ] . By improving understanding of complex cardiovascular flow patterns and integrating multimodal imaging data, these models provide a promising framework for patient-specific planning and comparison of surgical strategies [ 39 , 17 ] . These advantages are particularly relevant in congenital anatomies requiring complex biventricular repair, where surgical reconstruction must be tailored to highly patient-specific morphology and substantial anatomical heterogeneity can make preoperative planning difficult.

One particularly challenging defect is double outlet right ventricle (DORV), in which both the aorta and pulmonary artery arise predominantly from the right ventricle [ 15 ] . DORV is also characterized by the presence of a ventricular septal defect (VSD), a hole between the ventricles that results in mixing of oxygenated and deoxygenated blood. Successful repair often requires the construction of an intraventricular tunnel that redirects left ventricular blood flow through the VSD to the aorta while avoiding flow obstruction [ 19 ] . This is typically achieved using a surgically created intracardiac baffle, an internal patch or conduit that redirects blood flow within the heart [ 45 , 8 ] . However, the relationship of the VSD to the great arteries varies considerably across patients, making the feasibility and design of the baffle highly anatomy-dependent [ 7 ] .

Currently, baffles for DORV patients are designed and constructed intraoperatively on the arrested heart, requiring surgeons to shape and adjust the patch in real time while the heart is unpressurized [ 19 ] . This limits the ability to evaluate alternative configurations or quantitatively assess their hemodynamic performance preoperatively. Furthermore, the anatomical heterogeneity of DORV, variability in baffle design, and high rates of reoperation, further motivate the need for patient-specific computational tools to support surgical decision making.

To address these challenges, patient-specific surgical planning requires (i) a three-dimensional (3D) anatomical model derived from imaging data, (ii) a computational method for generating a patch that optimizes a defined objective, and (iii) a framework to evaluate the design using computational fluid dynamics (CFD). Patient-specific cardiac models can now be routinely reconstructed from CT or MRI, with recent machine learning approaches enabling rapid whole-heart segmentation and reducing reliance on manual workflows [ 25 , 47 , 34 ] . These models are used clinically for visualization, 3D printing, and surgical planning in complex congenital heart disease, including more recent virtual reality-based environments [ 5 , 71 , 58 , 9 , 24 ] . Major advances toward preoperative planning have been made through interactive visualization tools that allow clinicians to inspect patient-specific anatomy and define candidate baffle pathways within reconstructed models [ 66 ] . In some cases, these geometries are subsequently flattened to support operative template preparation, a has been demonstrated for branch pulmonary artery repair [ 33 ] . Other computational studies have examined the effects of idealized intraventricular tunnel geometries on pressure gradients, yet these rely on simplified or manually constructed conduits rather than automated patient-specific design workflows [ 69 ] . Thus, existing methods do not fully address key computational design requirements, including outflow tract area optimization, watertight integration with native anatomy, and CFD-based hemodynamic assessment.

This paper proposes a novel computational pipeline for generating patient-specific intraventricular baffles that redirect flow from the left ventricle to the aorta. Using systolic-phase cardiac computed tomograph (CT) images, the method produces a 3D baffle that integrates directly with the patient’s anatomical model for CFD analysis and surgical planning. The resulting baffle forms a tunnel from the ventricular septal defect (VSD) to the aortic valve while maintaining sufficient cross-sectional area along its length. We retrospectively applied this framework to four DORV patients who had previously undergone biventricular repair at our institution and compared the predicted pressure gradients from the virtual baffles with postoperative catheterization measurements from the surgically constructed pathways. These results support the feasibility of the method and its potential use in preoperative planning.


## 2 Methods

We present a computational method to generate and evaluate patient-specific intracardiac baffles that restore biventricular flow in congenital heart disease (CHD) patients. While this study focuses on DORV with VSDs in varied anatomical locations, this methodology is broadly applicable to other complex CHDs involving intracardiac flow rerouting. For each patient we performed the following steps: (1) reconstruction of a patient-specific 3D model from preoperative systolic CT imaging, (2) algorithmic generation of a closed intraventricular baffle surface, and (3) hemodynamic analysis of the reconstructed outflow pathway. ### 2.2 Computational Domain Preparation

This section details the workflow used to transform medical imaging data into segmentations and subsequently into a three-dimensional, multi-labeled mesh with an initial, unoptimized baffle.

For each of the four patients, the end-systolic phase from the preoperative CTA was extracted and segmented into seven anatomic structures. The systolic phase was selected for segmentation and subsequent baffle construction to capture ventricular geometry during peak ejection and better reflect the anatomy relevant to outflow reconstruction. The cardiac blood pools of the left ventricle (LV), right ventricle (RV), left atrium (LA), and right atrium (RA) were segmented to define the luminal portion of the heart, including trabeculations. The myocardial wall (myo) was segmented separately to distinguish tissue from blood pool. The pulmonary artery (PA) included the main pulmonary trunk and the proximal right and left branch PAs, while the aortic root (ao) consisted of the ascending aorta with the arch and branch vessels excluded.

The images were initially segmented using a U-Net–based deep learning framework. Convolutional neural networks have been well established for semantic segmentation objectives in a supervised setting with U-Net-based models remaining highly competitive [ 53 ] , especially in low-data regimes. We employed the self-configuring nnU-Net framework [ 26 ] , which automates preprocessing, training configuration, and hyperparameter selection. The model was trained on the ImageCHD dataset (67 training, 5 validation, 34 test cases) [ 68 ] and its performance was compared with a baseline U-Net implementation from SDF4CHD [ 34 ] . Training used default nnU-Net settings with DA5 data augmentation scheme, which applies extensive spatial and intensity transformations during training.

The resulting segmentations, along with the corresponding CT images, were then imported into 3D Slicer for manual refinement. The first column of Figure 6 illustrates the final seven-part segmentation for all four patients, with each row corresponding to a different patient. Mislabeled regions were corrected and unlabeled voxels were assigned to the appropriate anatomical class as needed, with particular attention to the ventricular septal interface and aortic valve annulus. All segmentations were reviewed and validated by a pediatric cardiologist with subspecialty training in cardiovascular imaging (Maskatia) to ensure accuracy and methodological consistency across patients.

The finalized multi-label segmentations were interpreted as three-dimensional image grids in which each voxel was assigned an integer label, ModelFaceID, corresponding to a specific anatomical structure. For each structure, a binary mask was generated by isolating voxels belonging to that label. To improve geometric and topological consistency, each binary mask underwent morphological closing and median filtering. Polygonal mesh, ℳ k \displaystyle\mathcal{M}_{k} , was extracted using the marching cubes algorithm, post-processed to remove topological artifacts (e.g., non-manifold edges), and geometrically smoothed [ 36 ] . For each subdomain mesh ℳ k \displaystyle\mathcal{M}_{k} , all triangular faces were assigned ModelFaceID = k \displaystyle k with mapping: LV =1 , RV = 2, LA = 3, RA = 4, myo = 5, ao = 6, PA = 7.

For each patient, we constructed a singular, manifold mesh, M AoBiV \displaystyle M_{\text{AoBiV}} , to represent the continuous blood pool of the LV, RV, and aorta by taking the union of the three segmented regions and following the same pipeline of filtering and surface extraction. This process automatically removed internal walls between constituent structures. We used the previously generated individual meshes { ℳ k } k ∈ { 1 , 2 , 6 } \displaystyle\{\mathcal{M}_{k}\}_{k\in\{1,2,6\}} as spatial references to assign anatomical labels to ℳ AoBiV \displaystyle\mathcal{M}_{\text{AoBiV}} . ModelFaceID was assigned to each triangular face in ℳ AoBiV \displaystyle\mathcal{M}_{\text{AoBiV}} based on the nearest anatomical surface, determined from the distance between the face centroid and the corresponding labeled regions.

Explicit boundary surfaces corresponding to the aortic valve annulus and the VSD were generated by truncating ℳ AoBiV \displaystyle\mathcal{M}_{\text{AoBiV}} using intersection contours between labeled anatomical subsurfaces. Specifically, the LV-RV and RV-aorta interfaces were used to define the VSD and aortic valve cutting loops, respectively. Intersection curves were computed using a vtkIntersectionPolyDataFilter. Due to mesh discretization, these raw curves were often irregular and non-planar. To regularize them, radial rays were cast from a provisional centroid onto a best-fit plane, and the first ray-surface intersection in each direction was retained. The resulting ordered surface points were smoothed to form closed, non-self-intersecting cutting loops.

ℳ AoBiV \displaystyle\mathcal{M}_{\text{AoBiV}} was then truncated along each loop using a surface selection (vtkSelectPolyData) followed by clipping operation (vtkClipPolyData), and the region between the two openings was retained. The resulting open boundaries were capped with the SimVascular VTMK capping operation to produce a watertight domain. These caps were planar by construction, yielding numerically well-posed inlet and outlet boundaries that approximated the local annular regions without attempting to reproduce the full non-planar anatomy. Each cap was assigned a unique ModelFaceID corresponding to the VSD inlet and aortic outlet. The final watertight, multi-labeled domain, ℳ cappedRV \displaystyle\mathcal{M}_{\text{cappedRV}} , formed the geometric basis for subsequent baffle construction consisting of the native right ventricular wall, the VSD inlet cap, and the aortic valve outlet cap. This preprocessing pipeline to obtain ℳ cappedRV \displaystyle\mathcal{M}_{\text{cappedRV}} is summarized in Figure 2 .

To determine the baffle attachment boundary, a congenital cardiac surgeon (Ma) interactively defined the intended suture line on the ventricular mesh using the BafflePlanner module in the SlicerHeart extension for 3D Slicer [ 35 , 66 ] . The suture line was specified by placing 12 − 15 \displaystyle 12-15 discrete points in three-dimensional space, which delineated a clinically feasible suture path along the endocardial surface of ℳ cappedRV \displaystyle\mathcal{M}_{\text{cappedRV}} . The resulting coordinate set served as both a visual guide and as the computational input for the following automated contour generation.

This sparse point set was converted into a densely sampled polyline by first projecting the points onto their two-dimensional principal component plane, where a greedy nearest-neighbor algorithm was used to establish a non-self-intersecting ordering. A parametric spline interpolant was then fit to the ordered points to generate a smooth 3D curve approximating the intended suture path. The spline was subsequently resampled at N = 600 \displaystyle N=600 equidistant parametric intervals, and each sampled point was projected back onto the surface of M cappedRV \displaystyle M_{\text{cappedRV}} using a closest-point projection. This produced the final dense, closed polyline defining the baffle boundary, ℬ baffle \displaystyle\mathcal{B}_{\text{baffle}} .

ℳ cappedRV \displaystyle\mathcal{M}_{\text{cappedRV}} was partitioned along the closed curve ℬ baffle \displaystyle\mathcal{B}_{\text{baffle}} , and the region containing both the VSD inlet and aortic outlet was retained. To obtain a watertight domain for subsequent steps, this opening was capped using SimVascular, creating an initial surface that served as a temporary placeholder. The boundary of this cap precisely defines the perimeter where the final baffle will be attached. The geometry was then fully remeshed in SimVascular to provide the geometric foundation for the baffle construction detailed in the following section. ### 2.3 Patient-Specific Baffle Surface Construction

With the boundary fixed, the remaining task was to determine the extrusion depth of the baffle into the RV. Starting from the initial placeholder surface, we constructed the final baffle surface 𝒮 baffle \displaystyle\mathcal{S}_{\text{baffle}} , and the integrated multi-surface domain ℳ final \displaystyle\mathcal{M}_{\text{final}} . The aim was to create a patient-specific intraventricular tunnel that achieved a prescribed target cross-sectional area, A target \displaystyle A_{\text{target}} , while maintaining a smooth, non-self-intersecting flow pathway. This was performed by enforcing area constraints along centerline-defined cross-sections and then applying smoothing.

We aimed to produce a VSD-to-aorta tunnel that maintains a physiologically appropriate cross-sectional area throughout. We defined A target \displaystyle A_{\text{target}} on a patient-specific basis to ensure that the resulting outflow geometry was consistent with normative values for body size. The native aortic annulus diameter ( d pt \displaystyle d_{\text{pt}} ) was measured directly from imaging. The Body Surface Area (BSA)-normalized reference annulus diameter at a z-score of 0 ( d z 0 \displaystyle d_{{z_{0}}} ) was obtained from established pediatric cardiology nomograms. The corresponding cross-sectional area was computed assuming a circular orifice. Normative values were derived using the Boston Children’s Hospital BSA-aortic annulus z-score calculator [ 14 , 59 ] , which is derived from measurements in normal children collected over approximately 12 years and follows established allometric adjustment methods. Anthropometric regressions (height, weight, BMI) use Centers for Disease Control and Prevention and World Health Organization reference datasets, and the echocardiographic z-score regressions are periodically updated as new normative models become available. We then defined a centerline along the intended VSD-to-aorta flow pathway to parameterize cross-sectional measurements. VMTK centerlines are derived from the Voronoi medial axis and follow minimal-action paths that maximize the inscribed radius [ 4 ] . This formulation is well suited for tubular vessels where the medial path coincides with the physiologic flow direction. In the intraventricular cavity, however, the lumen is wide and non-axisymmetric, and the VSD and aortic outlet may differ substantially in size. The resulting VMTK centerline would therefore follow the geometric medial path rather than the intended VSD-to-aorta trajectory and enter the inlet and outlet with poorly aligned tangents. Therefore, a custom centerline was constructed to enforce a smooth, physiologically aligned path with controlled endpoint tangents and stable normals for cross-sectional area evaluation.

However, directly applying these amplitudes produced high-frequency variations along the centerline, leading to local inward curvature between adjacent sections and a non-smooth baffle surface. These geometric irregularities created abrupt changes in cross-sectional area and surface normal direction, which are undesirable for smooth ventricular outflow. They are also mechanically unfavorable because surgically implanted baffles used in congenital cardiac reconstruction are commonly fabricated from autologous or bovine pericardium [ 60 , 2 ] . Pericardial tissue behaves as a thin, tension-dominated membrane and is thus susceptible to stress concentrations and material failure at sharp corners or abrupt bends [ 10 , 61 , 51 ] .

To mitigate these geometric irregularities, we imposed a convexity constraint on the amplitude sequence, yielding a smoother longitudinal profile. Specifically, given the discrete amplitudes { ( j , α j ) } j = 1 M \displaystyle\{(j,\alpha_{j})\}_{j=1}^{M} , we computed their upper convex envelope, defined as the smallest convex function lying above all points. This removed local dips in the amplitude profile while preserving section-wise area constraints. We defined two sets of three-dimensional target landmarks for the TPS interpolation: (i) the baffle attachment boundary ℬ baffle \displaystyle\mathcal{B}_{\text{baffle}} and (ii) the ordered set of final cross-sectional loops representing the desired baffle profile { ℒ ^ j } j = 1 M \displaystyle\{\hat{\mathcal{L}}_{j}\}_{j=1}^{M} . The final mesh consisted of the VSD inlet, aortic outlet, native right ventricular wall, and the constructed baffle surface, with each region uniquely identified by its ModelFaceID. The baffle surface, 𝒮 baffle \displaystyle\mathcal{S}_{\text{baffle}} , was extracted from ℳ final \displaystyle\mathcal{M}_{\text{final}} using this labeling while remaining an integral component of the full mesh.

Simulations were performed using SvMultiPhysics, a high-performance finite-element solver within the SimVascular software suite [ 72 ] . Beyond time step 100, these quantities exhibited a stable plateau with negligible temporal variation. ## 3 Results

The automated baffle generation and simulation workflow was applied on the pre-operateive image data of four DORV patients who had previously undergone biventricular repair with an intracardiac LV–Ao baffle. The resulting geometries, including the generated baffle surface 𝒮 baffle \displaystyle\mathcal{S}_{\text{baffle}} and reconstructed flow pathway ℳ final \displaystyle\mathcal{M}_{\text{final}} , were evaluated through hemodynamic simulations and compared with post-operative clinical measurements.


### 3.1 Final baffle surfaces and simulation-ready meshes

The workflow was successfully applied to all four DORV patient cases, producing high-fidelity, patient-specific surface models and simulation-ready computational meshes for each case. Across the cohort, the constructed baffles formed smooth, continuous surfaces that were free of self-intersections and integrate seamlessly with the native anatomy at the attachment boundary. In addition to meeting the prescribed geometric constraints, the resulting domains were watertight and topologically consistent, enabling robust downstream meshing and hemodynamic simulation. ## 4 Discussion

Computational modeling has emerged as an essential tool for patient-specific analysis of congenital heart defects, with applications spanning electrophysiology, electromechanics, solid mechanics, and fluid dynamics [ 22 , 13 , 52 , 55 , 63 , 57 ] . These approaches have increasingly been translated into clinical workflows to inform treatment planning and evaluate surgical strategies [ 20 , 70 , 39 ] . The complex anatomic and physiologic conditions associated with biventricular repair provide an ideal application for preoperative computational modeling.

Machine learning-based segmentation methods were successfully applied to accelerate generation of patient-specific 3D models for surgical planning. The nnU-Net-based pipeline reduced total segmentation time to roughly 1–2 hours per case compared to approximately 4–8 hours of conventional manual segmentation [ 46 ] . The most significant remaining limitations were in anatomically challenging regions, particularly near VSD margins and aortic valve annulus, where reduced image contrast and spatial resolution can limit accuracy [ 23 ] . These regions were therefore refined through expert congenital cardiac imaging review. As additional cases are incorporated into an expanded dataset, model performance is expected to continue to improve, further reducing the need for manual editing.

An important innovation of this workflow is that the user-defined baffle boundary is constrained to remain coincident with the underlying anatomical mesh, enabling robust generation of a closed, watertight geometry suitable for CFD analysis. In contrast to purely visualization-based design approaches, in which drawn curves may not remain fully attached to the surface, this mesh-conforming formulation preserves geometric consistency during tunnel construction. In the current implementation, the boundary was defined interactively by a congenital cardiac surgeon, who selects control points along the intended patch suture line in 3D Slicer. Although this approach enables incorporation of surgical intent, the resulting boundary remains user-dependent and directly influences the reconstructed tunnel geometry and predicted hemodynamics. Future work will focus on automated boundary selection based on geometric and hemodynamic optimization criteria, with the goal of identifying baffle paths that minimize pressure gradients while reducing inter-user variability.

To define the flow pathway, a custom centerline was constructed rather than using standard VMTK centerline extraction. VMTK methods assume a tubular lumen with comparable inlet and outlet size and generate a medial path that maximizes the inscribed radius [ 27 ] . In the intraventricular tunnel, however, the geometry is non-tubular and the inlet and outlet may differ in size and orientation. In the proposed approach, the centerline was defined using prescribed tangential segments at the inlet and outlet, which were then connected using a Piecewise Cubic Hermite Interpolating Polynomial (PCHIP). Although other interpolants could be applied, PCHIP was selected because it provided a shape-preserving interpolation, avoiding the overshoot commonly observed with cubic splines and producing stable cross-sectional normals near the boundaries [ 50 ] . When applying PCHIP, the lengths of the tangential transition regions at the inlet and outlet were set to one quarter of the local effective radius. Future work will optimize this parameter by estimating the transition lengths directly from the extent of the anatomically fixed regions adjacent to each boundary, thereby allowing the tangential segments to better reflect the curvature constraints imposed by the native geometry.

An initial, placeholder baffle was first generated, after which the PCHIP-based centerline defined cross-sectional planes spanning the combined ventricular domain. The resulting cross-sectional contours were modified by reshaping only the baffle segment of each loop using a parabolic displacement profile. Enforcing the target area independently at each section, however, produces locally irregular cross-sections with geometric depressions and abrupt transitions between adjacent slices. Such features are inconsistent with the smooth, low-curvature configurations typically assumed by autologous or bovine pericardial patches under physiological loading [ 60 , 2 ] . To address this, an upper convex envelope smoothing procedure was applied to the section-wise deformation amplitudes to generate a globally smooth outer envelope while preserving the prescribed lumen area. This approach removes local concavities and yields a monotonic, gently varying tunnel profile that better reflects expected surgical patch geometry and promotes more streamlined flow.

The final baffle surface was constructed using a thin-plate spline (TPS) formulation that interpolated the prescribed boundary curve and the smoothed cross-sectional profiles. TPS was selected because it produces a minimum-bending-energy surface with global C 2 \displaystyle C^{2} continuity, making it well suited for representing smoothly varying surgical patches while exactly satisfying boundary constraints [ 21 , 66 ] . Alternate surface construction methods were considered. Lofting between cross-sections is widely used for vascular reconstruction from ordered contours aligned along a centerline [ 65 , 70 ] . However, lofted surfaces are sensitive to contour correspondence and can exhibit slice-to-slice irregularities when applied to non-tubular geometries, particularly when contours are anisotropic [ 38 ] . Overall, TPS offers a robust balance between global smoothness and boundary conformity.

Although the proposed area-controlled profile combined with TPS produces a geometrically smooth and hemodynamically favorable surface, the optimal patch configuration is material-dependent and influenced by patch stiffness, fixation treatment, and compliance mismatch with the surrounding tissue [ 43 , 1 , 29 ] . Prior studies have demonstrated that suture-line constraints, axial pre-stretch, and differential compliance between patch material and native myocardium affect the implanted geometry and resulting stress distribution [ 33 ] . These effects are not explicitly represented in the present methodology, which treats the baffle as a purely geometric construct. Future work will incorporate material-informed constraints to better capture intraoperative deformation and postoperative morphology. In particular, coupling the geometric optimization with simplified structural models of patch mechanics and suture-line anchoring may improve prediction of the final implanted baffle shape.

Hemodynamic performance of the designed baffle was evaluated using SimVascular with steady-state CFD. Under steady-flow, rigid-wall assumptions, this framework provided an efficient first-order assessment of parabolic flow routing and the global pressure associated with directing VSD inflow toward the aortic outlet. Across patients, the converged inlet-to-outlet pressure differences were minimal, between 2 − 5 \displaystyle 2-5 mmHg, and well within accepted clinical ranges. These predicted gradients were consistently lower than the echocardiographic gradients measured for the implanted surgical baffles, by 3.04 to 9.88 mmHg, further supporting the potential use of this modeling framework to preoperatively identify baffle geometries with favorable hemodynamic performance. Despite this, there are localized regions with elevated pressure values that future studies could further evaluate and analyze.

Furthermore, cardiac blood flow occurs within a deforming ventricular domain. Rigid-wall models are unable to fully capture ventricular flow because ventricular contraction significantly influence intraventricular hemodynamics over the cardiac cycle. [ 12 , 62 ] . Despite this limitation, the proposed methodology produced physiologically reasonable flow pathways and pressure gradients across a spectrum of DORV anatomies suggesting that the geometric design framework is robust to underlying anatomical variability. Future work will incorporate FSI or coupled electromechanical and flow simulations with patient-specific ventricular kinematics and patch mechanics to better approximate the implanted baffle kinematics and hemodynamics through the outflow tract with cardiac wall motion. Prior work on multiscale, multiphysics whole-heart modeling supports the feasibility of such approaches [ 49 ] .

Although this study focused on biventricular planning in DORV, the proposed framework can be applied to other forms of intraventricular baffling and biventricular outflow reconstruction. The workflow is designed to generate a patient-specific baffle geometry for biventricular repair and evaluate resulting hemodynamic performance. Beyond DORV, these methods are intended to support surgical planning for biventricular conversion in borderline single-ventricle (SV) patients, a population characterized by significant anatomic and physiologic heterogeneity [ 6 ] . In this cohort, small variations in ventricular size, atrioventricular valve morphology, ventricular septal defect location, and outflow tract alignment can significantly influence the feasibility of biventricular repair and risk of post-operative obstruction. Patient-specific computational modeling provides a quantitative platform to evaluate candidate biventricular pathways, characterize hemodynamic trade-offs, and identify anatomically feasible configurations prior to intervention. We plan to use this framework to enable systematic comparison of repair strategies and support more objective, data-driven decision-making in these complex surgeries.


## 5 Conclusions

We presented a semi-automated computational framework for patient-specific design and evaluation of intraventricular baffles for biventricular repair. The method combines anatomical reconstruction, surgeon-guided boundary selection, centerline-based parameterization, and constrained surface generation to produce watertight, CFD-ready models. By preserving right ventricular geometry and reshaping only the baffle region, the framework constructs physiologically aligned VSD-to-aorta pathways that satisfy cross-sectional area targets along the tunnel. The final surface therefore not only spans the defect boundary smoothly but also respects a functional area constraint not addressed by simple boundary-based interpolation. Applied retrospectively to four DORV cases, this workflow generated anatomically conformal baffles with physiologically reasonable flow patterns and minimal pressure gradients between 2 – 5 mmHg, which were lower than the corresponding gradients measured by echocardiography after surgery. These results support the feasibility of the method as a preoperative planning tool that may help surgeons evaluate and optimize tunnel geometry before intervention with the potential to improve hemodynamic outcomes.
