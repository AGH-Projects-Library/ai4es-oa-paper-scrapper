## 4 Discussion

 Computational modeling has emerged as an essential tool for patient-specific analysis of congenital heart defects, with applications spanning electrophysiology, electromechanics, solid mechanics, and fluid dynamics [ 22 , 13 , 52 , 55 , 63 , 57 ] . These approaches have increasingly been translated into clinical workflows to inform treatment planning and evaluate surgical strategies [ 20 , 70 , 39 ] . The complex anatomic and physiologic conditions associated with biventricular repair provide an ideal application for preoperative computational modeling. Machine learning-based segmentation methods were successfully applied to accelerate generation of patient-specific 3D models for surgical planning. The nnU-Net-based pipeline reduced total segmentation time to roughly 1–2 hours per case compared to approximately 4–8 hours of conventional manual segmentation [ 46 ] . The most significant remaining limitations were in anatomically challenging regions, particularly near VSD margins and aortic valve annulus, where reduced image contrast and spatial resolution can limit accuracy [ 23 ] . These regions were therefore refined through expert congenital cardiac imaging review. As additional cases are incorporated into an expanded dataset, model performance is expected to continue to improve, further reducing the need for manual editing. An important innovation of this workflow is that the user-defined baffle boundary is constrained to remain coincident with the underlying anatomical mesh, enabling robust generation of a closed, watertight geometry suitable for CFD analysis. In contrast to purely visualization-based design approaches, in which drawn curves may not remain fully attached to the surface, this mesh-conforming formulation preserves geometric consistency during tunnel construction. In the current implementation, the boundary was defined interactively by a congenital cardiac surgeon, who selects control points along the intended patch suture line in 3D Slicer. Although this approach enables incorporation of surgical intent, the resulting boundary remains user-dependent and directly influences the reconstructed tunnel geometry and predicted hemodynamics. Future work will focus on automated boundary selection based on geometric and hemodynamic optimization criteria, with the goal of identifying baffle paths that minimize pressure gradients while reducing inter-user variability. To define the flow pathway, a custom centerline was constructed rather than using standard VMTK centerline extraction. VMTK methods assume a tubular lumen with comparable inlet and outlet size and generate a medial path that maximizes the inscribed radius [ 27 ] . In the intraventricular tunnel, however, the geometry is non-tubular and the inlet and outlet may differ in size and orientation. In the proposed approach, the centerline was defined using prescribed tangential segments at the inlet and outlet, which were then connected using a Piecewise Cubic Hermite Interpolating Polynomial (PCHIP). Although other interpolants could be applied, PCHIP was selected because it provided a shape-preserving interpolation, avoiding the overshoot commonly observed with cubic splines and producing stable cross-sectional normals near the boundaries [ 50 ] . When applying PCHIP, the lengths of the tangential transition regions at the inlet and outlet were set to one quarter of the local effective radius. Future work will optimize this parameter by estimating the transition lengths directly from the extent of the anatomically fixed regions adjacent to each boundary, thereby allowing the tangential segments to better reflect the curvature constraints imposed by the native geometry. An initial, placeholder baffle was first generated, after which the PCHIP-based centerline defined cross-sectional planes spanning the combined ventricular domain. The resulting cross-sectional contours were modified by reshaping only the baffle segment of each loop using a parabolic displacement profile. Enforcing the target area independently at each section, however, produces locally irregular cross-sections with geometric depressions and abrupt transitions between adjacent slices. Such features are inconsistent with the smooth, low-curvature configurations typically assumed by autologous or bovine pericardial patches under physiological loading [ 60 , 2 ] . To address this, an upper convex envelope smoothing procedure was applied to the section-wise deformation amplitudes to generate a globally smooth outer envelope while preserving the prescribed lumen area. This approach removes local concavities and yields a monotonic, gently varying tunnel profile that better reflects expected surgical patch geometry and promotes more streamlined flow. The final baffle surface was constructed using a thin-plate spline (TPS) formulation that interpolated the prescribed boundary curve and the smoothed cross-sectional profiles. TPS was selected because it produces a minimum-bending-energy surface with global C 2 \displaystyle C^{2} continuity, making it well suited for representing smoothly varying surgical patches while exactly satisfying boundary constraints [ 21 , 66 ] . Alternate surface construction methods were considered. Lofting between cross-sections is widely used for vascular reconstruction from ordered contours aligned along a centerline [ 65 , 70 ] . However, lofted surfaces are sensitive to contour correspondence and can exhibit slice-to-slice irregularities when applied to non-tubular geometries, particularly when contours are anisotropic [ 38 ] . Overall, TPS offers a robust balance between global smoothness and boundary conformity. Although the proposed area-controlled profile combined with TPS produces a geometrically smooth and hemodynamically favorable surface, the optimal patch configuration is material-dependent and influenced by patch stiffness, fixation treatment, and compliance mismatch with the surrounding tissue [ 43 , 1 , 29 ] . Prior studies have demonstrated that suture-line constraints, axial pre-stretch, and differential compliance between patch material and native myocardium affect the implanted geometry and resulting stress distribution [ 33 ] . These effects are not explicitly represented in the present methodology, which treats the baffle as a purely geometric construct. Future work will incorporate material-informed constraints to better capture intraoperative deformation and postoperative morphology. In particular, coupling the geometric optimization with simplified structural models of patch mechanics and suture-line anchoring may improve prediction of the final implanted baffle shape. Hemodynamic performance of the designed baffle was evaluated using SimVascular with steady-state CFD. Under steady-flow, rigid-wall assumptions, this framework provided an efficient first-order assessment of parabolic flow routing and the global pressure associated with directing VSD inflow toward the aortic outlet. Across patients, the converged inlet-to-outlet pressure differences were minimal, between 2 − 5 \displaystyle 2-5 mmHg, and well within accepted clinical ranges. These predicted gradients were consistently lower than the echocardiographic gradients measured for the implanted surgical baffles, by 3.04 to 9.88 mmHg, further supporting the potential use of this modeling framework to preoperatively identify baffle geometries with favorable hemodynamic performance. Despite this, there are localized regions with elevated pressure values that future studies could further evaluate and analyze. Furthermore, cardiac blood flow occurs within a deforming ventricular domain. Rigid-wall models are unable to fully capture ventricular flow because ventricular contraction significantly influence intraventricular hemodynamics over the cardiac cycle. [ 12 , 62 ] . Despite this limitation, the proposed methodology produced physiologically reasonable flow pathways and pressure gradients across a spectrum of DORV anatomies suggesting that the geometric design framework is robust to underlying anatomical variability. Future work will incorporate FSI or coupled electromechanical and flow simulations with patient-specific ventricular kinematics and patch mechanics to better approximate the implanted baffle kinematics and hemodynamics through the outflow tract with cardiac wall motion. Prior work on multiscale, multiphysics whole-heart modeling supports the feasibility of such approaches [ 49 ] . Although this study focused on biventricular planning in DORV, the proposed framework can be applied to other forms of intraventricular baffling and biventricular outflow reconstruction. The workflow is designed to generate a patient-specific baffle geometry for biventricular repair and evaluate resulting hemodynamic performance. Beyond DORV, these methods are intended to support surgical planning for biventricular conversion in borderline single-ventricle (SV) patients, a population characterized by significant anatomic and physiologic heterogeneity [ 6 ] . In this cohort, small variations in ventricular size, atrioventricular valve morphology, ventricular septal defect location, and outflow tract alignment can significantly influence the feasibility of biventricular repair and risk of post-operative obstruction. Patient-specific computational modeling provides a quantitative platform to evaluate candidate biventricular pathways, characterize hemodynamic trade-offs, and identify anatomically feasible configurations prior to intervention. We plan to use this framework to enable systematic comparison of repair strategies and support more objective, data-driven decision-making in these complex surgeries.
