## 1 Introduction

 Congenital heart defects (CHDs) are the most common type of birth defect, affecting roughly 40,000 births annually in the United States. They encompass a broad and highly variable spectrum of structural abnormalities [ 11 ] . Many patients with severe defects have only one ventricle effectively contributing to cardiac output and therefore require surgical intervention to restore adequate circulation. Historically, these highly heterogeneous patients were often palliated to a single-ventricle physiology [ 32 ] . However, this strategy is associated with substantial long-term morbidity and mortality. Therefore, when anatomically feasible, several advanced centers offer complex biventricular repair as an alternative strategy that restores two-ventricle physiology [ 16 , 3 ] . In parallel, computational modeling has emerged as a powerful tool for generating patient-specific predictions across a range of clinical applications [ 41 , 28 , 44 , 48 , 54 ] , and supporting clinical decision making, with particular impact in pediatric cardiology [ 64 , 30 , 73 , 39 , 17 , 40 , 31 , 31 ] . By improving understanding of complex cardiovascular flow patterns and integrating multimodal imaging data, these models provide a promising framework for patient-specific planning and comparison of surgical strategies [ 39 , 17 ] . These advantages are particularly relevant in congenital anatomies requiring complex biventricular repair, where surgical reconstruction must be tailored to highly patient-specific morphology and substantial anatomical heterogeneity can make preoperative planning difficult. One particularly challenging defect is double outlet right ventricle (DORV), in which both the aorta and pulmonary artery arise predominantly from the right ventricle [ 15 ] . DORV is also characterized by the presence of a ventricular septal defect (VSD), a hole between the ventricles that results in mixing of oxygenated and deoxygenated blood. Successful repair often requires the construction of an intraventricular tunnel that redirects left ventricular blood flow through the VSD to the aorta while avoiding flow obstruction [ 19 ] . This is typically achieved using a surgically created intracardiac baffle, an internal patch or conduit that redirects blood flow within the heart [ 45 , 8 ] . However, the relationship of the VSD to the great arteries varies considerably across patients, making the feasibility and design of the baffle highly anatomy-dependent [ 7 ] . Currently, baffles for DORV patients are designed and constructed intraoperatively on the arrested heart, requiring surgeons to shape and adjust the patch in real time while the heart is unpressurized [ 19 ] . This limits the ability to evaluate alternative configurations or quantitatively assess their hemodynamic performance preoperatively. Furthermore, the anatomical heterogeneity of DORV, variability in baffle design, and high rates of reoperation, further motivate the need for patient-specific computational tools to support surgical decision making. To address these challenges, patient-specific surgical planning requires (i) a three-dimensional (3D) anatomical model derived from imaging data, (ii) a computational method for generating a patch that optimizes a defined objective, and (iii) a framework to evaluate the design using computational fluid dynamics (CFD). Patient-specific cardiac models can now be routinely reconstructed from CT or MRI, with recent machine learning approaches enabling rapid whole-heart segmentation and reducing reliance on manual workflows [ 25 , 47 , 34 ] . These models are used clinically for visualization, 3D printing, and surgical planning in complex congenital heart disease, including more recent virtual reality-based environments [ 5 , 71 , 58 , 9 , 24 ] . Major advances toward preoperative planning have been made through interactive visualization tools that allow clinicians to inspect patient-specific anatomy and define candidate baffle pathways within reconstructed models [ 66 ] . In some cases, these geometries are subsequently flattened to support operative template preparation, a has been demonstrated for branch pulmonary artery repair [ 33 ] . Other computational studies have examined the effects of idealized intraventricular tunnel geometries on pressure gradients, yet these rely on simplified or manually constructed conduits rather than automated patient-specific design workflows [ 69 ] . Thus, existing methods do not fully address key computational design requirements, including outflow tract area optimization, watertight integration with native anatomy, and CFD-based hemodynamic assessment. This paper proposes a novel computational pipeline for generating patient-specific intraventricular baffles that redirect flow from the left ventricle to the aorta. Using systolic-phase cardiac computed tomograph (CT) images, the method produces a 3D baffle that integrates directly with the patient’s anatomical model for CFD analysis and surgical planning. The resulting baffle forms a tunnel from the ventricular septal defect (VSD) to the aortic valve while maintaining sufficient cross-sectional area along its length. We retrospectively applied this framework to four DORV patients who had previously undergone biventricular repair at our institution and compared the predicted pressure gradients from the virtual baffles with postoperative catheterization measurements from the surgically constructed pathways. These results support the feasibility of the method and its potential use in preoperative planning.
