 To evaluate the hemodynamics of our final designs, we simulated three-dimensional, steady-state blood flow within the final patient-specific domain ℳ final \displaystyle\mathcal{M}_{\text{final}} . All simulations were conducted using the same consistent set of numerical and fluid-property parameters. Blood flow was modeled as an incompressible, Newtonian fluid governed by the Navier-Stokes equations: [[TABLE_0]]  where Ω \displaystyle\Omega is the fluid domain defined by ℳ final \displaystyle\mathcal{M}_{\text{final}} , 𝐮 \displaystyle\mathbf{u} is the fluid velocity vector, and p \displaystyle p is the pressure. The fluid density was set to ρ = 1.06 ​ g/cm 3 \displaystyle\rho=1.06\text{g/cm}^{3} , and the dynamic viscosity to μ = 0.04 \displaystyle\mu=0.04 Poise (g/(cm ⋅ s ) ) \displaystyle\text{Poise (g/(cm}\cdot\text{s})) [ 42 ] . Patient-specific hemodynamic parameters were extracted from preoperative echocardiograms. The inflow rate was calculated from the left ventricular outflow tract velocity–time integral (LVOT VTI) and cardiac output (CO). The computational domain boundary was divided into three regions: the VSD inlet, the aortic outlet, and rigid walls, comprising the native ventricular wall and the baffle surface. A parabolic velocity profile was prescribed at the inlet to enforce the patient-specific inflow rate Q in \displaystyle Q_{\text{in}} , while a zero-velocity Dirichlet condition was applied on the rigid walls. At the outlet, a resistance boundary condition was imposed to represent the downstream vasculature. The resulting inflow rates and outlet resistances are summarized in Table 3 . ![Table 3: Patient-Specific Hemodynamic Parameters]() [[TABLE_1]]  Simulations were performed using SvMultiPhysics, a high-performance finite-element solver within the SimVascular software suite [ 72 ] . The coupled velocity-pressure system was discretized using a stabilized finite-element formulation and solved with a preconditioned GMRES iterative solver, with FSILS used as the preconditioner and a relative tolerance of 10 − 5 \displaystyle 10^{-5} . The simulation was conducted over 150 time steps with a time step size of 0.001 sec to obtain a steady-state solution. Convergence was assessed by monitoring face-integrated fluxes at the inlet, outlet, and wall boundaries. Beyond time step 100, these quantities exhibited a stable plateau with negligible temporal variation. The corresponding change in pressure was less than 0.005 mmHg between successive time steps, indicating that a steady solution had been reached.
