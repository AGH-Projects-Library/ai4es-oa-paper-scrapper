 With the boundary fixed, the remaining task was to determine the extrusion depth of the baffle into the RV. Starting from the initial placeholder surface, we constructed the final baffle surface ЁЭТо baffle \displaystyle\mathcal{S}_{\text{baffle}} , and the integrated multi-surface domain тД│ final \displaystyle\mathcal{M}_{\text{final}} . The aim was to create a patient-specific intraventricular tunnel that achieved a prescribed target cross-sectional area, A target \displaystyle A_{\text{target}} , while maintaining a smooth, non-self-intersecting flow pathway. This was performed by enforcing area constraints along centerline-defined cross-sections and then applying smoothing. We aimed to produce a VSD-to-aorta tunnel that maintains a physiologically appropriate cross-sectional area throughout. We defined A target \displaystyle A_{\text{target}} on a patient-specific basis to ensure that the resulting outflow geometry was consistent with normative values for body size. The native aortic annulus diameter ( d pt \displaystyle d_{\text{pt}} ) was measured directly from imaging. The Body Surface Area (BSA)-normalized reference annulus diameter at a z-score of 0 ( d z 0 \displaystyle d_{{z_{0}}} ) was obtained from established pediatric cardiology nomograms. The corresponding cross-sectional area was computed assuming a circular orifice. Normative values were derived using the Boston ChildrenтАЩs Hospital BSA-aortic annulus z-score calculator [ 14 , 59 ] , which is derived from measurements in normal children collected over approximately 12 years and follows established allometric adjustment methods. Anthropometric regressions (height, weight, BMI) use Centers for Disease Control and Prevention and World Health Organization reference datasets, and the echocardiographic z-score regressions are periodically updated as new normative models become available. VMTK centerlines are derived from the Voronoi medial axis and follow minimal-action paths that maximize the inscribed radius [ 4 ] . This formulation is well suited for tubular vessels where the medial path coincides with the physiologic flow direction. In the intraventricular cavity, however, the lumen is wide and non-axisymmetric, and the VSD and aortic outlet may differ substantially in size. The resulting VMTK centerline would therefore follow the geometric medial path rather than the intended VSD-to-aorta trajectory and enter the inlet and outlet with poorly aligned tangents. Therefore, a custom centerline was constructed to enforce a smooth, physiologically aligned path with controlled endpoint tangents and stable normals for cross-sectional area evaluation. We constructed a smooth three-dimensional centerline connecting inlet and outlet caps using a Piecewise Cubic Hermite Interpolating Polynomial (PCHIP) spline with normal-aligned endpoint ramps. The raw amplitudes { ╬▒ j } \displaystyle\{\alpha_{j}\} obtained from the cross-sectional optimization ensured that each reconstructed loop individually satisfied the target area constraint. However, directly applying these amplitudes produced high-frequency variations along the centerline, leading to local inward curvature between adjacent sections and a non-smooth baffle surface. These geometric irregularities created abrupt changes in cross-sectional area and surface normal direction, which are undesirable for smooth ventricular outflow. They are also mechanically unfavorable because surgically implanted baffles used in congenital cardiac reconstruction are commonly fabricated from autologous or bovine pericardium [ 60 , 2 ] . Pericardial tissue behaves as a thin, tension-dominated membrane and is thus susceptible to stress concentrations and material failure at sharp corners or abrupt bends [ 10 , 61 , 51 ] . To mitigate these geometric irregularities, we imposed a convexity constraint on the amplitude sequence, yielding a smoother longitudinal profile. Specifically, given the discrete amplitudes { ( j , ╬▒ j ) } j = 1 M \displaystyle\{(j,\alpha_{j})\}_{j=1}^{M} , we computed their upper convex envelope, defined as the smallest convex function lying above all points. This removed local dips in the amplitude profile while preserving section-wise area constraints.
