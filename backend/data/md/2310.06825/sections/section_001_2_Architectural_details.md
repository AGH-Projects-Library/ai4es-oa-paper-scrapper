SWA exploits the stacked layers of a transformer to attend information beyond the window size W 𝑊 W . The hidden state in position i 𝑖 i of the layer k 𝑘 k , h i subscript ℎ 𝑖 h_{i} , attends to all hidden states from the previous layer with positions between i − W 𝑖 𝑊 i-W and i 𝑖 i . Recursively, h i subscript ℎ 𝑖 h_{i} can access tokens from the input layer at a distance of up to W × k 𝑊 𝑘 W\times k tokens, as illustrated in Figure 1 . At the last layer, using a window size of W = 4096 𝑊 4096 W=4096 , we have a theoretical attention span of approximately 131 ​ K 131 𝐾 131K tokens. Rolling Buffer Cache. A fixed attention span means that we can limit our cache size using a rolling buffer cache. The cache has a fixed size of W 𝑊 W , and the keys and values for the timestep i 𝑖 i are stored in position i mod W modulo 𝑖 𝑊 i\bmod W of the cache. As a result, when the position i 𝑖 i is larger than W 𝑊 W , past values in the cache are overwritten, and the size of the cache stops increasing. We provide an illustration in Figure 2 for W = 3 𝑊 3 W=3 . On a sequence length of 32k tokens, this reduces the cache memory usage by 8x, without impacting the model quality. ![Figure 2: Rolling buffer cache. The cache has a fixed size of W = 4 𝑊 4 W=4 . When generating a sequence, we need to predict tokens one-by-one, as each token is conditioned on the previous ones. However, the prompt is known in advance, and we can pre-fill the ( k 𝑘 k , v 𝑣 v ) cache with the prompt. If the prompt is very large, we can chunk it into smaller pieces, and pre-fill the cache with each chunk. For this purpose, we can select the window size as our chunk size. For each chunk, we thus need to compute the attention over the cache and over the chunk.
